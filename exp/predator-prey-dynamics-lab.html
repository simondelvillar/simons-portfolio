Reading the Mathematics

Fixed points

Setting \(x_{n+1}=x_n\) and \(y_{n+1}=y_n\) gives two equilibria: extinction \((0,0)\), and coexistence \(\left(\frac{b}{c}, \frac{r}{a}\right)\) — the only population levels at which both species exactly replace themselves each step.

Stability

Linearizing the map at a fixed point gives a Jacobian whose eigenvalue magnitudes decide its fate: if both \(|\lambda| < 1\) the point attracts nearby trajectories; if any \(|\lambda| > 1\) the equilibrium repels, and small perturbations grow.

Oscillation

Complex eigenvalues at the coexistence point produce rotation in phase space — the classic predator-lag cycle, where predator peaks trail prey peaks because growth in each population only feeds through to the other on the next step.

Sensitivity

Because the map is nonlinear, small changes in \(r\), \(a\), \(b\), or \(c\) can shift the system between damped settling, sustained cycles, and runaway divergence or collapse — discrete time steps make this transition sharper than in the continuous Lotka–Volterra analogue.
